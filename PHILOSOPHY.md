# The Philosophy of This Tool
## A Living Document

---

This tool does not produce truth.
It produces structured thinking about claims.
The judgment is always yours.
These principles exist to temper and inform that judgment — not to replace it.

---

## What This Is

A reality calibration platform built on the Examined Uncertainty principle.

Most tools that assess claims pretend to deliver verdicts. This one refuses to.
The goal is not to tell you what to believe. It is to show you what you are already
assuming, what the evidence actually supports, and what a more honest picture
of a complicated situation looks like.

If it works, you leave with better questions — not settled answers.

---

## Core Philosophical Commitments

**Examined Uncertainty**
Most important things cannot be known with certainty. The honest response to this
is not to fake certainty, nor to collapse into "everything is equally valid."
It is to hold uncertainty explicitly — to know what you don't know, to map the
possibility space rather than prematurely collapsing it, and to act wisely anyway.

**Probabilistic over Binary**
Claims are not true or false. They have confidence levels that shift with evidence.
LOW confidence does not mean false. HIGH confidence does not mean certain.
The tiers are honest about what the evidence actually supports.

**Assumptions First**
Every assessment rests on assumptions. Making those assumptions visible is more
valuable than the verdict itself. When you know what you'd have to believe for
something to be true, you can examine whether you actually believe it.

**Generative over Dismissive**
An unfalsifiable claim is not worthless. A conspiracy theory is not purely delusion.
A metaphysical position is not automatically nonsense. The honest response is to
ask: what is real in this? What legitimate concern or genuine insight does it contain?
Then assess what the evidence actually supports from there.

## On Scientific Consensus

Consensus is one input. It is not ground truth.

The replication crisis has shown that mainstream fields — psychology, medicine,
social science — produce unreliable results at rates that should make everyone
more humble about what "the science says."

Institutional rejection of a claim is not the same as the evidence being weak.
These are different things and must be treated differently.

"Shut up and calculate" — the deliberate avoidance of the philosophical and
metaphysical implications of empirical theories — is a documented failure mode
of institutional science. When a field does this, it deserves to have it named.

## On Human Conflict

Every conflict is between people who are all, to varying degrees, products of
circumstances they did not choose, trying to protect something they genuinely
value, often unable to see that the other side is doing the same thing.

This is not a both-sides-are-equal claim.
Power asymmetries are real.
Some positions are more defensible than others.
Some actors are more corrupt than others.

But it is the starting orientation that makes productive engagement possible
rather than just sophisticated condemnation.

**The loudest voices are not the median voices.**
In any group, the most extreme positions receive the most algorithmic amplification.
The median member of any movement is almost always more reasonable than their
most visible representatives. This tool tries to assess the median, not the loudest.

**Incentive structures corrupt symmetrically.**
Any movement, channel, or institution that depends on conflict for its survival
will systematically produce and amplify conflict — regardless of whether the
underlying concern is legitimate. This applies to all sides of every dispute.
It is flagged symmetrically here or not at all.

**Shared ground exists beneath most apparent oppositions.**
Most bitter conflicts are fought between people who share the majority of their
values and are fighting over a smaller contested remainder — often without knowing
about the 80% they agree on. Making the shared ground visible changes the
emotional register of the whole conversation.

## On Moral Judgment

No one is simply good or simply bad.

Everyone looks monstrous at their worst moment and admirable at their best.
The honest unit of moral assessment is the pattern across a life — the arc,
the trajectory, the ratio of harm to care across time and circumstances.

The free will question is load-bearing in moral assessment and must not be
treated as settled. Whether a person had genuine access to better choices —
given their trauma history, neurological wiring, available knowledge, and
circumstance — is genuinely uncertain. Moral confidence should vary accordingly.

Compassion for a person who caused harm does not require suspending judgment
about the harm. Both can be held simultaneously. The most morally mature
position is often exactly that — holding compassion and accountability together
without collapsing one into the other.

## On the Limits of This Tool

This tool is powered by a language model trained on human-generated text.
That training data has biases, gaps, a knowledge cutoff, and blind spots
that cannot be fully known or corrected for.

The assessed confidence tiers are structured outputs, not computed probabilities.
The numbers and categories are frameworks for thinking, not measurements.

The tool cannot replace:
- Primary source research
- Domain expertise
- Your own lived experience and judgment
- Conversations with people who disagree with you

It can help you:
- See what assumptions you're making
- Find the strongest version of opposing views
- Identify what evidence would actually change your mind
- Approach contested questions with more intellectual honesty

---

## A Note on Improvement

This framework is itself improvable.

The principles here were developed through conversation and iteration.
They will be wrong in places. They will have blind spots.
They will need updating as the tool encounters new kinds of claims
and new kinds of failure.

If you find a place where the tool's reasoning is less honest, less nuanced,
or less fair than it should be — that is useful information.
The examined uncertainty principle applies to this framework itself.

No algorithm produces truth.
No heuristic is final.
The judgment is always yours.
